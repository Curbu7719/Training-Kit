# Worked Example: A Safe Hour with the AI Assistant

It's a normal morning and you reach for the AI assistant a few times. Here's how a few small habits keep each use safe and still useful.

**Summarise a long email thread.** You paste a 40-message thread and ask for the key decisions. *Safe move:* the thread has a customer's name and order details, so you use the **approved platform** (not a random chatbot), and you skim the summary against the real messages before forwarding it — because a summary can quietly drop or invent a decision. *Why use AI?* It saves 20 minutes of reading; you keep the 2 minutes of checking.

**Ask a policy question.** "What's our refund window?" The AI answers "30 days," confidently. *Safe move:* you treat it as a **draft** and check the real policy page — the model may be guessing from old training data, not your current policy. It was actually 14 days. That check just stopped you from telling a customer the wrong thing.

**Draft a status update.** You ask it to write your weekly update from your notes. *Safe move:* your notes hold no PII and no secret, so it's fine to send; you read the draft and fix the one overstated line before posting. You own what you post.

**A ticket tries to hijack the AI.** While summarising a bug ticket, the AI suddenly suggests emailing an internal config file out. *Safe move:* you stop — that instruction came from the *ticket text* (**prompt injection**), not from you. You don't act on it, and you flag it.

**The takeaway:** using AI safely isn't extra work — it's a few habits. Approved tool, no sensitive data, treat answers as drafts, verify what counts, and never act on instructions hidden in content. You get the speed and skip the incident.
