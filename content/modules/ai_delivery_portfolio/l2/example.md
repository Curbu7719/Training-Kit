# Worked Example: Steer a Portfolio When AI Made Building Cheap

You've run one AI-driven project well; now you're looking across many initiatives that all build with AI assistants, competing for the same budget, review capacity, and leadership attention. AI lowered the cost of *starting* things — and that's exactly what makes portfolio discipline matter more, not less. Here's how steering it makes the cheap-to-build era a strength instead of a sprawl.

**Cheap to build is a trap without discipline.** When each initiative is fast and cheap to start, more ideas become viable — and you end up with a sprawl of half-finished, lightly-reviewed bets. *Why does this make your day easier to get right?* You score every initiative the same way — value, feasibility, risk — so the cheap start doesn't smuggle low-value work into the portfolio. The constraint moved from engineering capacity to **prioritisation and judgement**.

**Keep real kill discipline.** The cost of *starting* dropping doesn't make a low-value bet worth *finishing*. *Why use AI this way?* Because the sunk-cost trap now wears a disguise — "but the AI already built most of it." Killing it still frees the scarce resource that AI *didn't* make cheaper: review capacity and leadership attention.

**Standardise what repeats.** Across many AI-driven teams, the same guardrails, review gates, and patterns recur. *Why does this matter?* Standardising them once means every initiative inherits safe defaults instead of each team reinventing (or skipping) them — that's leverage only a portfolio view can capture.

**Invest where the new constraint is.** Spend less on "can we build it" (AI mostly answered that) and more on review capacity, evaluation, and prioritisation. *Why?* That's where throughput is now gated — funding the old constraint just produces more unreviewed code.

**The takeaway:** AI didn't remove portfolio discipline — it raised the stakes on it. Score and kill consistently, standardise the repeats, and invest in the new bottleneck (review and judgement), so a cheaper-to-build world becomes more good software shipped, not more half-built bets to maintain.
