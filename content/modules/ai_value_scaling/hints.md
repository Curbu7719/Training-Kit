# Hints — AI Value & Scaling

## Alternative phrasings of the core idea

- AI value is the business outcome (time, money, risk, quality) the system produces — not the
  fact that it uses AI; scaling is the disciplined path from a proven pilot to wide use.
- A real value metric ties to a business result; a vanity metric (prompts run, raw accuracy)
  looks good but does not. Always ask: if this number doubled, would the business be better off?
- The lifecycle is pilot → production → scale, and the scale-or-kill decision rests on a
  pre-agreed value threshold, real adoption, and sustainable cost — killing a weak pilot is a win.

## Hint stack

- **H1 (nudge):** Separate "looks impressive" from "moves the business." Drafts generated is
  the first; handle-time saved that agents actually accept is the second.
- **H2 (structural):** Order the lifecycle by purpose: a small time-boxed test with a success
  metric (pilot), then a monitored owned system (production), then extending to more
  users/use cases (scale). Decide to scale or kill *between* pilot and production.
- **H3 (near-answer):** Scale only when the value metric clears its pre-agreed threshold,
  adoption is real, and cost is sustainable. If those fail, killing the pilot frees budget —
  that is a successful outcome, not a failure.

## FAQ

**Q: Isn't high model accuracy proof of value?**
No. Accuracy is a vanity metric unless it ties to an outcome. A 99%-accurate model nobody
uses, or that saves no time, produces zero business value. Measure the outcome, not the model.

**Q: Why would killing a pilot ever be a good result?**
Because a pilot is an experiment. Stopping one that has not proven value frees budget and
attention for bets that do work. Most pilots should not graduate — that is the point of
piloting cheaply first.

**Q: We built it and it works — why isn't anyone getting value?**
Likely an adoption gap. Technology nobody uses creates zero value. Change management —
training, trust, clear workflows — is what converts a working system into realised value.
Measure active and repeat users, not just availability.

**Q: When is a pilot ready to scale?**
When its value metric clears the threshold you agreed *before* the pilot, adoption is real,
and the cost to run it at higher volume stays below the benefit it delivers.
